MODULE 16·Modern Bihar

Battle of Plassey &
Battle of Buxar

Two battles, seven years apart — both fought on or near Bihar's soil. Plassey made the British powerful in Bengal; Buxar made them rulers of India. The Treaty of Allahabad (1765) that followed gave the EIC the Diwani rights over Bihar, Bengal and Orissa — the foundation of British India.

BPSC Special Mains GS-I ⚠ Trap: Buxar fought 22 Oct 1764 (NOT 1757). Diwani granted 12 Aug 1765. Shitab Rai = first Naib Deewan of Bihar. Golghar built 1786 for famine of 1783.
🎭Story HookSTART HERE

The year is 22 October 1764. On a battlefield at Buxar, Bihar, three armies face the British: the forces of Mir Qasim (ex-Nawab of Bengal), Shah Alam II (the Mughal Emperor himself), and Shuja-ud-Daula (Nawab of Awadh). Together, they represent the last real military challenge to British rule in India.

By evening, they are all defeated. Shah Alam II — the Mughal Emperor — flees, is later captured, and is forced to sign a treaty granting the British revenue control over Bihar, Bengal, and Orissa in exchange for an annual pension and two districts. The Mughal Empire, which had once stretched from Kabul to the Bay of Bengal, is reduced to a pensioner of the East India Company.

This is why historians say: Plassey made the British wealthy; Buxar made them the rulers of India.

💡 The mighty Mughal Empire ended not with a bang but a firman — Shah Alam II's signature on the Treaty of Allahabad (12 August 1765) handed Bihar, Bengal and Orissa to the EIC forever.
🧠Core Concept — Two Battles, Full CoverageWITH ANALOGY
⚔️ Modern Analogy

Think of Plassey as a hostile corporate takeover — the British bribed the company's own CFO (Mir Jafar) to betray the CEO (Siraj-ud-Daula). Think of Buxar as the legal battle that followed — when the shareholders (Shah Alam II, Mir Qasim, Shuja-ud-Daula) tried to reverse the takeover and lost. After Buxar, it wasn't just a takeover — it was a complete acquisition with full ownership rights (Diwani).

1757 AD

Battle of Plassey — Bihar Connection

Fought 23 June 1757. Robert Clive + Mir Jafar (conspiratorial) vs Siraj-ud-Daula. Siraj-ud-Daula defeated; Mir Jafar made new Nawab of Bengal. Bihar Effect: After Plassey, British seized all French warehouses in Patna. Bihar still not fully under British control. Most Bihar decisions taken from Bengal. Raja Ram Narayan was Bihar's deputy governor — didn't participate in Plassey conspiracy; felt insecure.

1760–1763 AD

Mir Qasim — Moves Capital to Munger, Bihar

1760: British replaced Mir Jafar with his son-in-law Mir Qasim as Nawab of Bengal. Mir Qasim transferred capital from Murshidabad to Munger (Bihar) — chosen for strategic position between Bengal and Bihar. Built gun factory at Munger. Conflict with British over misuse of Dastaks (free trade passes) by EIC traders.
Battle of Udwanala (2 Sep 1763): British defeated Mir Qasim. Captured Munger and Patna. Mir Qasim executed Raja Ram Narayan (drowned in Ganga). Fled to Awadh → secured support of Shah Alam II and Shuja-ud-Daula.

22 Oct 1764 ⚠

Battle of Buxar — Decisive British Victory

Date: 22 October 1764 — fought at Buxar, Bihar. British army led by Major Hector Munro vs combined army of Mir Qasim + Shah Alam II (Mughal Emperor) + Shuja-ud-Daula (Nawab of Awadh). British won decisively. Shuja-ud-Daula's army collapsed at Kora (3 May 1765). Mir Qasim fled; died in poverty in Delhi (1777). Shah Alam II captured/surrendered.

12 Aug 1765 ⚠

Treaty of Allahabad — Diwani Rights Granted

Two Treaties of Allahabad signed by Robert Clive:
1st Treaty (12 August 1765) with Shah Alam II: Emperor granted EIC Diwani rights (revenue collection) of Bengal, Bihar and Orissa. In return: EIC gave Shah Alam II districts of Kora and Allahabad + Rs 26 lakhs annual tribute.
2nd Treaty (16 August 1765) with Shuja-ud-Daula: Awadh restored to him; paid Rs 50 lakhs war indemnity.
Result: EIC = virtual masters of Bihar, Bengal and Orissa.

1765–1772 AD

Dyarchy in Bihar — Clive to Hastings

Robert Clive introduced Dyarchy (1765): EIC held Diwani (revenue/civil administration); Nawab held Nizamat (law and order). Bihar's first Naib Deewan = Shitab Rai (appointed by Clive). Warren Hastings abolished Dyarchy (1772) — direct Company administration began. After Shitab Rai's death (Sep 1773), son Kalyan Singh made Deewan by Hastings.
1783 Famine → Hastings ordered construction of Golghar granary in Patna. Designed by John Garstin. Construction completed 28 July 1786.

Causes of Buxar

Mir Qasim's Reforms vs EIC

Mir Qasim abolished Dastaks to stop EIC private trade. EIC traders refused. War broke out. Mir Qasim allied with Shah Alam II + Shuja-ud-Daula.

Battle (22 Oct 1764)

British Win at Buxar

Hector Munro defeats all three combined armies. Last united resistance to British rule in India crushed. Most decisive battle of the 18th century.

Result: Diwani 1765

Bihar-Bengal-Orissa = EIC Revenue

Treaty of Allahabad grants EIC Diwani of Bihar, Bengal, Orissa. Mughal Emperor becomes pensioner. British become de facto rulers of east India.

📝PYQ PracticeSOLVED
BPSC 2008
When did Shah Alam II grant the Diwani Right of Bengal and Bihar to the English East India Company?
(a) 12th August, 1765
(b) 18th August, 1765
(c) 29th August, 1765
(d) 21st August, 1765
✓ CORRECT: (a) 12th August, 1765

Shah Alam II granted the Diwani rights of Bengal and Bihar (and Orissa) to the EIC on 12 August 1765 via the Treaty of Allahabad. The second Treaty of Allahabad with Shuja-ud-Daula was signed on 16 August 1765. Know both dates — BPSC tests them separately.

BPSC 2001
Which among the following Indians was appointed as Naib Deewan (Deputy Deewan) of Bihar by Robert Clive?
(a) Omi Chand
(b) Manik Chand
(c) Rai Durlabh
(d) Shitab Rai
✓ CORRECT: (d) Shitab Rai

Robert Clive appointed Shitab Rai as the Naib Deewan (Deputy Deewan) of Bihar after the Treaty of Allahabad (1765). Shitab Rai became the most powerful Indian official in Bihar during the Dyarchy period. He was later suspended on corruption charges but acquitted. After his death (September 1773), Warren Hastings appointed his son Kalyan Singh as Deewan of Bihar.

BPSC MCQ
To cope with famines of Bihar, the granary of Golghar was built in:
(a) 1786
(b) 1770
(c) 1745
(d) 1760
✓ CORRECT: (a) 1786

Golghar construction was completed on 28 July 1786. It was ordered by Warren Hastings after the terrible famine of 1783 (not 1770 — that was a different famine). Designed by John Garstin, the dome-shaped granary on the banks of the Ganga in Patna was built to store grain for future famines. It stands as a symbol of early British infrastructure in Bihar.

MAINS GS-I · 150 WORDS
Why is the Battle of Buxar (1764) considered more significant than the Battle of Plassey (1757) in the history of Bihar and British India? Discuss with reference to the Treaty of Allahabad.
Introduction

While Plassey (1757) established British dominance in Bengal, it was Buxar (1764) that transformed the East India Company from a trading company into the sovereign power of India's richest provinces.

Why Buxar was More Significant
  • Scale: Buxar involved three armies — Mir Qasim, Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II, and Nawab Shuja-ud-Daula. Plassey involved only one Nawab who was already betrayed
  • Legal Outcome: Plassey gave no formal rights; Buxar led to the Treaty of Allahabad granting EIC Diwani (revenue rights) over Bihar, Bengal AND Orissa
  • Bihar Specific: Buxar was fought on Bihar's soil; Plassey was in Bengal. Bihar entered British administration directly from Buxar
  • Mughal Capitulation: Shah Alam II's signature on the Treaty of Allahabad (12 Aug 1765) symbolised the end of Mughal political authority in India
Treaty of Allahabad — Bihar's Fate

The 12 August 1765 treaty gave EIC full Diwani rights, making Shitab Rai the first Naib Deewan of Bihar. The subsequent Dyarchy (1765–72), abolished by Warren Hastings, completed Bihar's transition to direct British administration.

Conclusion

As historians note, "Plassey made the British wealthy; Buxar made them rulers of India." For Bihar specifically, Buxar was the definitive moment — after 1764, Bihar's revenue, administration, and political future were irreversibly in British hands.
