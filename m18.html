MODULE 18·Modern Bihar

Tribal Revolts
of Bihar

Before the sepoys mutinied in 1857, Bihar's tribes had been fighting British colonialism for nearly a century. From the Nonia saltpetre workers of 1770 to Birsa Munda's Ulgulan of 1900 — these were India's first organised anti-colonial movements, driven by land, forest, and dignity.

BPSC Special Mains GS-I ⚠ Note: Most tribal revolt areas are now in Jharkhand (formed 2000). Still Bihar History syllabus.
🎭Story Hook

The year is 1900. In Chota Nagpur, a 25-year-old Munda man named Birsa Munda has declared himself "Dharti Abba" (Father of the Earth). Thousands follow him. His message: the forests belong to the tribes, the land belongs to the tribes, and the rule of the British — and their local agents the zamindars — must end.

He is captured on 3 February 1900. On 9 June 1900, he dies in jail of cholera, aged just 25. But his revolt forces the British to pass the Chota Nagpur Tenancy Act (11 November 1908) — one of the most important pieces of tribal land legislation in Indian history.

This is the pattern of every tribal revolt in Bihar: the British suppress it militarily, but the tribes always extract a policy concession. Each revolt left behind a law, a district, or a protected area. The forests may have lost the battles; they won the laws.

💡 All six major tribal revolts listed here preceded the 1857 national revolt — making Bihar's tribes India's FIRST anti-colonial fighters. Birsa Munda's face is today on India's ₹10 coin — a tribal freedom fighter's ultimate recognition.
🧠All Major Tribal Revolts — Complete Coverage
Nonia Revolt1770–1800 AD
  • Tribe: Nonias — caste entrusted with collecting saltpetre soil
  • Area: Hajipur, Tirhut, Saran, Purnea
  • Cause: Mediators bought raw saltpetre from Nonias at low prices, sold to factories at high prices. EIC oppressed Nonias when they tried to sell directly to other traders.
  • Significance: One of Bihar's earliest organised anti-British economic revolts
Kol Uprising1831–1832 AD
  • Leader: Budhu Bhagat — most prominent leader
  • Area: Chota Nagpur (Ranchi, Singhbhum, Hazaribagh, Palamu, W. Manbhum)
  • Cause: Transfer of tribal lands to outsiders (Sikh and Muslim farmers/thikadars); non-tribal flood into Kol villages; abduction of Kol women; police corruption
  • Immediate cause: Abduction of wife of Surya Munda of Bardgaon by outsider Jafar Ali Khan
  • Other leaders: Sindrai, Surga, Bindrai Maniki, Soee Bhadur
  • Outcome: New South East Frontier Agency created; large-scale military operation
Bhumij Revolt1832–1833 AD
  • Leader: Ganga Narayan Singh
  • Area: Singhbhum + Birbhum (mainly)
  • Cause: British land revenue increase; exploitation by King of Veerbhum, Munsifs, Zamindars
  • Start: 26 April 1832 — Diwan of Veerbhum murdered
  • End: Ganga Narayan Singh killed in battle on 7 February 1833 by Thakur Chetan Singh
Santhal Uprising (Hul)1855–1856 AD
  • Leaders: Sidhu and Kanhu (brothers)
  • Area: Rajmahal Hills — spread to Birbhum, Bankura, Hazaribagh
  • Cause: Rapacious moneylenders, land transfer to outsiders, exploitative zamindars, corrupt police. Impossible to get British court relief. Sidhu and Kanhu first tried constitutional means (petitions, marches) — failed.
  • Declared independence (1854): Santhals declared end of Company rule and themselves independent
  • Start of armed revolt: July 1855 at Rajmahal hills
  • End: Sidhu died/killed 1855; Kanhu arrested 1866
  • Outcome: Santhal Pargana created; Ashley Aiden became its first District Collector
Munda Revolt (Ulgulan)1899–1900 AD
  • Leader: Birsa Munda — called "Dharti Abba" (Father of the Earth)
  • Area: Chota Nagpur (mainly Ranchi area)
  • Ulgulan = "rebellion" or "great tumult" in Munda language
  • Cause: Forest Rights Act 1865 and Indian Forest Act 1878 took away Munda forest rights; land alienation to thikadars and moneylenders; Sardari movement not enough
  • Nature: Religious, agrarian AND political — aimed to establish "Munda Raj" by killing thikadars, jagirdars, rajas, hakims
  • Birsa gathered force of 6000 Mundas
  • Birsa captured: 3 February 1900
  • Birsa died in jail: 9 June 1900 — cholera; aged ~25
  • Outcome: Chota Nagpur Tenancy Act — 11 November 1908; Mundari Khuntkatti system recognised
Tana Bhagat Movement1914 AD
  • Leader: Jatra Bhagat — other leaders: Balram Bhagat, Bhikhu Bhagat, Shibu Bhagat
  • Tribe: Oraon tribe of Chota Nagpur
  • Nature: Essentially religious; considered extension of Birsa Movement
  • Method: Non-violence — followers of Mahatma Gandhi; accepted Ahimsa
  • Objective: Against landlords, contractors, taxes imposed by British
  • Bhagat = distinct section of Oraon tribe subscribing to Bhakti cult
  • Movement incorporated Hindu practices into tribal ideology

📝PYQ Practice
BPSC MCQ
The Kol Uprising (1831–32) was led by:
(a) Budhu Bhagat
(b) Sidhu and Kanhu
(c) Birsa Munda
(d) Raja Parhat
✓ CORRECT: (a) Budhu Bhagat

Budhu Bhagat led the Kol Uprising of 1831–32 in Chota Nagpur against the expansion of British rule and transfer of tribal lands to outsiders (Sikh and Muslim farmers). The revolt covered Ranchi, Singhbhum, Hazaribagh, Palamu and western Manbhum. Two other leaders were Sindrai and Surga who continued fighting till 1832.

BPSC MCQ
Birsa Munda was captured on:
(a) 3 February 1900
(b) 9 June 1900
(c) 11 November 1908
(d) 15 August 1900
✓ CORRECT: (a) 3 February 1900

Birsa Munda was captured on 3 February 1900. He died in jail on 9 June 1900 due to cholera. Know both dates: captured = 3 Feb 1900; died = 9 Jun 1900. Option (c) 11 November 1908 = Chota Nagpur Tenancy Act passed as outcome of his revolt. Note: option (b) is his death date, not capture date.

BPSC MCQ
What was created by the British to pacify the Santhal Uprising?
(a) Specific reserved area
(b) Santhal Pargana
(c) Autonomous status
(d) None of the above
✓ CORRECT: (b) Santhal Pargana

After the Santhal Uprising (1855–56) by Sidhu and Kanhu, the British created the Santhal Pargana as a specially administered district. Ashley Aiden became its first District Collector. This was a concession to tribal demands for their own administrative area — the classic British pattern of military suppression + policy concession.

✅Concept Check
1. "Ulgulan" is associated with which tribal revolt?
✓ Correct! "Ulgulan" means "rebellion" or "great tumult" in the Munda language and specifically refers to the Munda Revolt (1899–1900) led by Birsa Munda. It was a religious, agrarian and political movement aimed at establishing "Munda Raj" by ending the rule of thikadars, jagirdars, rajas, hakims and the British.
✗ Answer is (C) Munda Revolt. "Ulgulan" = rebellion in Munda language = Birsa Munda's 1899–1900 revolt. The result was the Chota Nagpur Tenancy Act (11 November 1908) — one of the most important tribal land laws in India.
2. The Tana Bhagat Movement (1914) was started by which tribe?
✓ Correct! The Tana Bhagat Movement (1914) was started by the Oraon tribe of Chota Nagpur, led by Jatra Bhagat. It is considered an extension of the Birsa Movement. It was essentially religious in nature, non-violent (Gandhian/Ahimsa), and directed against landlords, contractors, and British taxes.
✗ Answer is (C) Oraon tribe. Tana Bhagat Movement (1914) = Oraon tribe + Jatra Bhagat leader. Know the tribe-revolt mapping: Santhal → Sidhu-Kanhu; Munda → Birsa; Oraon → Jatra Bhagat.
MAINS GS-I · 150 WORDS
Analyse the causes and consequences of the Santhal Uprising (1855–56) and the Munda Revolt (1899–1900). How did these revolts contribute to tribal rights legislation?
Introduction

The Santhal Uprising (1855–56) and Munda Revolt (1899–1900) stand as the two most significant tribal revolts in Bihar's colonial history — both rooted in land alienation, and both ultimately producing crucial legislative protections for tribal communities.

Common Causes
  • Land alienation to non-tribal outsiders (dikus, thikadars, moneylenders)
  • Exploitative British forest laws (Indian Forest Act 1878 for Mundas)
  • Rapacious moneylenders whose debt traps dispossessed tribals
  • Failure of British courts to provide justice to tribal complainants
Santhal Uprising (1855–56)
  • Led by Sidhu and Kanhu from Rajmahal Hills; spread to Birbhum, Bankura, Hazaribagh
  • Tribals declared end of Company rule (1854) — explicitly anti-colonial
  • Tried constitutional means first (petitions, marches) — failed; then armed revolt
  • Outcome: Santhal Pargana created as separate district; Ashley Aiden = first DC
Munda Revolt/Ulgulan (1899–1900)
  • Led by Birsa Munda ("Dharti Abba") — religious, agrarian, political combined
  • Birsa captured 3 Feb 1900; died in jail 9 Jun 1900 (cholera, age ~25)
  • Outcome: Chota Nagpur Tenancy Act (11 Nov 1908) — most significant tribal rights law; Mundari Khuntkatti system recognised
Conclusion

Both revolts demonstrate that colonial courts ignored tribal petitions but responded to armed revolts with legislation. The pattern — military suppression followed by policy concession — repeated across Bihar's tribal revolts. Today, the Fifth Schedule of the Constitution and PESA Act continue the legislative tradition these revolts pioneered.
