MODULE 10·Medieval Bihar

Turkish Invasion &
Slave Dynasty in Bihar

In 1197–1203 AD, a single military campaign ended 700 years of Buddhist scholarship. Bakhtiyar Khalji's destruction of Nalanda, Vikramshila, and Odantapura was not just a military event — it was a civilisational rupture that changed Bihar forever.

BPSC Prelims Mains GS-I 1197 AD – 1236 AD ⚠ Trap: Bakhtiyar Khalji ≠ Qutbuddin Aibak. Bakhtiyar was Qutbuddin's general, NOT the Delhi Sultan himself
🎭Story HookSTART HERE

The year is 1197 AD. A Tibetan scholar who survived the destruction later wrote: "The Turks came at midnight. They thought it was a fort — because the monks had shaved heads, they assumed them to be soldiers. They killed everyone they found."

What the Turks had attacked was Nalanda University — the world's greatest library. The monastery burned for three months. The monastery at Vikramshila burned next. Then Odantapura. In less than a decade, Muhammad Bakhtiyar Khalji had erased 700 years of Buddhist civilisation in Bihar.

One Tibetan monk, Dharmasvamin, visiting in 1234 AD, found just 70 students remaining at Nalanda, taught by a 90-year-old teacher in a crumbling wing. The great university was a ruin. Its books, most of them, were ash.

💡 The books burned so long at Nalanda that Muslim soldiers reportedly used the embers to cook their food for three months — a detail that captures the scale of what was lost.
🧠Core Concept — Bakhtiyar Khalji & Slave DynastyWITH ANALOGY
⚔️ Modern Analogy

Imagine all of India's IITs, IIMs, and libraries being destroyed in one decade. That is what Bakhtiyar Khalji did to Bihar. But this wasn't random vandalism — it was strategic. By destroying Buddhist monasteries (which doubled as the administrative infrastructure of the Sena and Pala states), he simultaneously eliminated religious opposition AND the knowledge base that supported local governance. It was devastatingly effective.

~1197–1203 AD

Bakhtiyar Khalji — First Muslim Conqueror of Bihar

Muhammad Bin Bakhtiyar Khalji was a military general of Qutbuddin Aibak (the Delhi Sultan). He was NOT the sultan himself. His invasions of Bihar occurred in phases:
• ~1197 AD: Attacked Maner and then Hisar-i-Bihar (Bihar Sharif) — plundered Odantapuri Mahavihara. Monks were mistaken for soldiers (shaved heads); massacred.
• ~1199 AD: Destroyed Nalanda University — library burned for 3 months.
• ~1203 AD: Destroyed Vikramshila Mahavihara at Antichak, Bhagalpur.
• Reduced Sena rulers to vassals. Defeated Narsimhadeva of Karnata dynasty (Tirhut).
• Founded the city of Bakhtiyarpur (1198 AD).
• Was killed by his own lieutenant Ali Mardan Khalji. Buried at Bihar Sharif (Imadpur Muhalla).

1225 AD

Iltutmish — First Sultan to Formally Control Bihar

Iltutmish (slave of Qutbuddin Aibak) became the real consolidator of Slave Dynasty rule in Bihar. In 1225 AD, he captured Bihar Sharif — making him the first Sultan to organise a military campaign and get possession of Bihar under Delhi Sultanate. He appointed Malik Alauddin Jani as first Subedar (Governor) of Bihar. Later Tughrul Tughan became governor. After Iltutmish's death (1236 AD), central control over Bihar weakened.

1236–1286 AD

Balban — Order Restored in Bihar

Balban became sultan and appointed his son Nasir-ud-din Bughra Khan as governor of Bihar (1282-83 AD). He was presented with 63 elephants by Tartar Khan (Bihar's governor) — symbol of Bihar's revenue/military importance. Balban defeated and killed Tughril Khan (Lakhnauti ruler) and brought Bihar under tighter control. Bihar Sharif, Bhojpur, Gaya, Patna, Munger, Bhagalpur, Nalanda, Lakhisarai — all under Slave Dynasty.

💥 Impact of Turkish Invasion on Bihar

Destruction of Buddhism

Nalanda (427–1197 AD), Vikramshila, Odantapura all destroyed. Buddhism as a living tradition in Bihar effectively ended.

Knowledge Loss

Centuries of manuscripts, commentaries, and Buddhist texts burned. Only texts that scholars had carried abroad (to Tibet, China) survived.

Political Change

Bihar went from independent Hindu/Buddhist kingdoms to part of Delhi Sultanate (1200–1526 AD). A permanent political transformation.

New Administrative Order

Sultanate administrative system — Subedars, revenue farming, new laws — replaced indigenous governance systems.

✅Concept CheckTEST YOURSELF
1. Who was the real founder of Turkish rule (first Muslim conqueror) in Bihar?
✓ Correct! Muhammad Bin Bakhtiyar Khalji was the first Muslim conqueror of Bihar. He was a general (not sultan) — serving under Qutbuddin Aibak. He sacked Nalanda, Vikramshila, and Odantapuri. He established Bakhtiyarpur and shifted his base to Lakhnauti. He was killed by Ali Mardan and his mausoleum is in Bihar Sharif.
✗ Answer is (A) Muhammad Bin Bakhtiyar Khalji. He was the GENERAL of Qutbuddin Aibak (not the sultan himself) who personally conquered Bihar. Iltutmish consolidated control later (1225 AD) but Bakhtiyar was the first conqueror.
2. Odantapuri educational institution (Bihar Sharif) was located in which state?
✓ Correct! Odantapuri (also written Odantapura or Uddandapura) was located at Bihar Sharif in the Nalanda district of Bihar. It was founded by Gopala (first Pala king) around 750 AD. It was the first of the three great Buddhist universities to be destroyed by Bakhtiyar Khalji (~1197 AD).
✗ Answer is (B) Bihar. Odantapuri was at Bihar Sharif, Nalanda district, Bihar. This is a BPSC 2017 question. The town "Bihar Sharif" itself is named after the Bihar (Vihara = monastery) at Odantapuri — reinforcing how central this site was to Bihar's identity.
MAINS GS-I150 words
Assess the impact of Bakhtiyar Khalji's invasion on Bihar's cultural and political landscape. Was it merely a military event or a civilisational rupture?
Introduction

Muhammad Bin Bakhtiyar Khalji's invasions (1197–1203 AD) were far more than military campaigns. They ended Bihar's 1,700-year unbroken tradition of Buddhist scholarship and permanently altered the region's religious, cultural, and political identity.

Scale of Destruction
  • Three great universities — Nalanda (founded 427 AD), Vikramshila (founded 770 AD), Odantapura (founded 750 AD) — all destroyed within a decade
  • Nalanda's library burned for 3 months; centuries of manuscripts turned to ash
  • Only texts carried abroad (Tibet, China) survived — creating a diaspora of Indian Buddhist knowledge
Why It Was a Civilisational Rupture
  • Religious: Buddhism as a living tradition in Bihar ended; Hinduism became dominant
  • Political: Destroyed the administrative infrastructure of the Pala/Sena states (monasteries doubled as records offices and revenue centres)
  • Educational: No comparable institution replaced these universities in Bihar for centuries
  • Cultural Memory: The trauma shaped how Bihari identity was constructed in subsequent centuries
Conclusion

Khalji's raids were a civilisational rupture — not merely a regime change. The modern revival of Nalanda University (2014) as Nalanda International University is an attempt to reconnect with this severed tradition, over 800 years later.
