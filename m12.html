MODULE 12·Medieval Bihar

Sher Shah Suri —
From Sasaram to All of India

Born near Sasaram, Bihar. Rose from a jagirdar's son to Bihar's master to Emperor of all India in just two decades. Ruled only 5 years — yet built the Grand Trunk Road, created the silver Rupee, reformed land revenue, and laid the administrative blueprint that even the mighty Mughals copied.

BPSC Special Mains GS-I ⚠ Trap: Sher Shah belongs to Sasaram (NOT Patna). Died at Kalinjar (NOT Sasaram). Tomb at Sasaram.
🎭Story HookSTART HERE

The year is 1540 AD. The Mughal Emperor Humayun — son of Babur, heir to the greatest military dynasty in Asia — is running. He has thrown himself into the Ganges at Chausa, Bihar, and was saved only by a water-carrier named Nizam Saqqa. Now, after his defeat at the Battle of Kannauj, he is fleeing to Persia. He will not return to India for 15 years.

The man who drove him out? A 54-year-old Afghan from Sasaram, Bihar named Farid Khan — who earned the title "Sher Khan" (Lion King) by killing a tiger with his bare hands. Who rose from a minor jagirdar's assistant to master of Bihar to master of all India in just 15 years.

In his 5 short years on the Delhi throne, Sher Shah would build roads the British would still use in the 20th century, introduce a currency system that lasted until 1947, and create an administrative machinery so efficient that even Akbar studied and copied it. He is, perhaps, medieval India's most underrated ruler — and he was Bihar's own.

💡 The word "Rupee" — used by 1.5 billion Indians today — was introduced by Sher Shah Suri of Bihar in 1540 AD. A man from Sasaram created India's most enduring financial institution.
🧠Rise of Sher Shah — Step by StepWITH ANALOGY
🚀 Modern Analogy

Sher Shah is like a startup founder who joined a company as an intern, learned everything, took over the company, then disrupted the entire industry. He started as a tutor's son in Sasaram → became an administrator under Noohanis → took over Bihar → drove out Humayun → reformed all of India in 5 years. His reforms were so good, Akbar's administration literally copied them (Raja Todarmal's land revenue system is adapted from Sher Shah's Zabt system).

EARLY LIFE

Farid Khan — Son of Hasan Khan Sur, Jagirdar of Sasaram

Born ~1486 AD at Narnaul (Hisar-Firoza) according to Dr. Qanungo. Father Hasan Khan Sur was jagirdar of Sasaram, Bihar. Farid grew up near Sasaram — associated with Sasaram, Bihar. Given title "Sher Khan" by Sultan Muhammad (Noohani) for killing a tiger. Appointed jagirdar of Sasaram AND tutor to Jalal Khan (last Noohani).

1534 AD

Battle of Surajgarh (Munger) — Decisive Victory

Nuhani nobles jealous of Sher Shah sought help from Bengal's Nusrat Shah. Forces met at Surajgarh (in Munger district). Sher Khan won decisively. Took title "Hazrat-e-Ala". Became virtual master of Bihar. Considered one of the most decisive battles in medieval Indian history.

STRATEGY

Chunar Fort — Obtained by Marriage

Obtained strategic Chunar Fort by marrying Lado Malka, widow of Taj Khan Sarangkhani. This gave him control of the Ganga highway between Bihar and Agra — a military masterstroke. Expanded influence to Gaur (Bengal capital) and beyond Munger.

1539 AD — BATTLE OF CHAUSA

Humayun Routed — Sher Khan Becomes Sher Shah

Chausa (east of Karmanasa, 4 miles west of Buxar, Bihar). Sher Khan made surprise attack on June 26, 1539. Humayun threw himself into Ganga — saved by water-carrier Nizam Saqqa. After this victory: Sher Khan took the title "Shah" → became Sher Shah. Occupied Bihar and Bengal.

1540 AD — BATTLE OF KANNAUJ (BILGRAM)

Humayun Expelled from India

Battle at Bilgram (near Kanauj), 1540 AD. Sher Shah thoroughly defeated Humayun — forced him to leave India and seek refuge in Persia. Sher Shah became undisputed Emperor of India. Made Patna capital of Bihar province (1541 AD). Shifted capital from Bihar Sharif to Patna.

MAY 13, 1545

Death at Kalinjar Fort — The Tragic End

Sher Shah was besieging Kalinjar Fort (Bundelkhand). A rocket accidentally exploded near the gunpowder magazine. Sher Shah was mortally wounded. Died May 13, 1545. Ruled for only 5 years (1540–1545) but achieved more than most rulers do in 50. Tomb at Sasaram (Rohtas district), Bihar — one of India's finest Afghan-era architectural monuments.

🔄Sher Shah's Reforms — ComprehensiveALL SECTIONS TESTED
ADMINISTRATIVE

🏛️ Sarkar (District) System

Introduced Sarkar (district) as administrative unit for first time in India. Under Sikedra-e-Sikderana. Divided empire into provinces → Sarkars → Parganas → Villages. This framework lasted through Mughal and British rule.

REVENUE

📊 Zabt Land Revenue System

Revived land measurement as basis of revenue (after Alauddin Khalji's time). Called Zabt system (later perfected by Todarmal under Akbar). Revenue taken in cash based on crop price list "Rai." First king to give farmers Patta (right over their land). Additional taxes: Zaribana (measurement duty) + Muhislana (collection duty). Revenue increased 4-fold.

CURRENCY

🪙 Silver Rupee — India's Currency

Introduced silver Rupee (Rupai) of 178 grains — uniform, standard coin replacing the chaotic multi-metal currency. This silver rupee system lasted (with modifications) until India adopted decimal currency in 1957. The word "Rupee" = Sher Shah's legacy to modern India.

INFRASTRUCTURE

🛣️ Grand Trunk Road

Extended Grand Trunk Road from Kabul (Afghanistan) to Chittagong (Bangladesh) — 2,400 km. Planted shade trees along it. Built Sarais (rest houses) every 2 kos (1 kos ≈ 3 km). These sarais doubled as postal relay stations. Called "Sadak-e-Azam" or "Badshahi Sadak." British renamed it "Grand Trunk Road."

MILITARY

⚔️ Military Reforms

Revived the Dagh (branding of horses) and Huliya (description of soldiers) systems. Prevented fraud in army rolls. Maintained large standing army. Built strategic forts — especially Rohtasgarh in Bihar where he transferred Bengal's treasures.

PATNA / BIHAR

🏙️ Bihar Connections

Born in Bihar region (Sasaram). Made Patna capital of Bihar (1541 AD). Built fort at Patna. Monuments at Sasaram (tomb), Patna, Hilsa, Telhara, Shergarh. Son Islam Shah continued his legacy (ruled 1545–1553). Tarikh-i-Shershahi by Abbas Sarvani = key source.

BPSC PrelimsBPSC 2013
Sher Shah Suri belongs to which place?
(a) Patna
(b) Gaya
(c) Sasaram
(d) Arrah
✓ CORRECT: (c) Sasaram

Sher Shah Suri belongs to Sasaram (Rohtas district, Bihar). His father Hasan Khan Sur was the jagirdar of Sasaram. Though he was born at Narnaul (Hisar-Firoza per Dr. Qanungo), his formative career and identity are associated with Sasaram. His magnificent tomb is also at Sasaram — the finest example of Afghan-era architecture in India. He shifted Bihar's capital from Bihar Sharif to Patna (1541 AD) — so Patna is his administrative capital, but Sasaram is his hometown.

BPSC PrelimsBPSC 2014
The tomb of Sher Shah is built in:
(a) Sasaram
(b) Kalinjar
(c) Chausa
(d) Patna
✓ CORRECT: (a) Sasaram

⚠️ Common trap: Sher Shah died at Kalinjar (during the siege), but his tomb is at Sasaram (Rohtas district, Bihar). The tomb, built in the middle of an artificial lake, is considered the finest example of Indo-Afghan architecture in India. Kalinjar = place of death; Sasaram = tomb location. Know this distinction.

BPSC PrelimsBPSC 2008
In Bihar, where was the headquarters of Sher Shah?
(a) Gaya
(b) Darbhanga
(c) Patna
(d) Saran
✓ CORRECT: (c) Patna

Sher Shah made Patna the capital (headquarters) of Bihar province in 1541 AD. He built a fort at Patna and shifted from Bihar Sharif (the previous capital under Slave and Tughlaq dynasties) to Patna. This decision was strategically sound — Patna's position at the Ganga-Sone confluence made it the perfect administrative and military hub. This is the origin of Patna as Bihar's undisputed capital.

Introduction

Sher Shah Suri (1540–45 AD), a son of Bihar's Sasaram, achieved in 5 years what most medieval rulers could not in 50 — creating an administrative, revenue, and infrastructural framework that his Mughal successors themselves adopted wholesale.

Bihar-Specific Contributions
  • Patna as Capital (1541): Shifted Bihar's capital from Bihar Sharif to Patna — establishing Patna's permanent role as Bihar's administrative heart
  • Battle of Chausa (1539): Fought on Bihar soil (near Buxar), this battle made a Bihari ruler Emperor of India
  • Tomb at Sasaram: Greatest architectural monument of medieval Bihar; an enduring symbol of Bihari cultural heritage
National Contributions
  • Silver Rupee (178 grains): India's standardised currency for 400+ years
  • Grand Trunk Road (Kabul–Chittagong): 2,400 km; still operational as NH-19
  • Zabt Land Revenue System: Measurement-based revenue with Patta rights for farmers; 4x revenue increase; adopted by Akbar via Todarmal
  • Sarkar (District) System: Provincial → District → Pargana structure; became permanent administrative template
Conclusion

Sher Shah earned the historian's description as "predecessor of Akbar" — a Bihar-born ruler who gave India its first modern administrative system, its enduring currency, and its greatest pre-colonial road. Five years of governance, five centuries of legacy.
