MODULE 01 · Ancient Bihar

Sources &
Prehistoric Bihar

Before a single word was written, Bihar was already alive — in stone tools, painted cave walls, bone instruments, and ancient river settlements. This module decodes how historians reconstruct Bihar's story from silence.

BPSC Prelims Mains GS-I ~2500 BC – 600 BC ⚠ Trap: No Paleolithic remains found in Bihar
🎭 Story Hook START HERE

The year is 1912. A British archaeologist named David Brainard Spooner is digging near a crumbling mound called Bulandi Bagh on the outskirts of Patna. His shovel strikes something unusually smooth — not rock, not brick. It is a polished sandstone pillar, one of 80, each carved from stone quarried 200 km away at Chunar and transported to form a magnificent columned hall.

With that single strike, 2,300 years of silence ended. Spooner had uncovered the 80-pillared assembly hall of Chandragupta Maurya at Kumhrar — proof that ancient Pataliputra was not legend but real. Bihar's history, it turned out, had been waiting underground all along.

But how do historians piece together even older stories — from when there were no pillars, no inscriptions, no coins? The answer lies in four brilliant detective tools: spades, stones, words, and travellers.

💡 Memory Hook: Bihar's name itself comes from "Vihara" (Buddhist monastery) — named by 12th century Muslim invaders who saw monasteries everywhere.
🧠 Core Concept WITH ANALOGY
📱 Modern Analogy

Think of historical sources like recovering a deleted phone. Archaeological finds = the hardware (physical evidence). Inscriptions/coins = saved files. Literary sources = screenshots others took. Foreign accounts = friends who visited and wrote reviews. Together they reconstruct the full picture — even what was "deleted."

🏺 Archaeological Sources

Physical remains: ruins, tools, pottery, inscriptions on stone/copper, coins. Key Bihar sites: Kumhrar (Mauryan hall), Chirand (Neolithic bones), Paisra/Munger (Stone Age tools), Vaishali/Nalanda (Buddhist remains). Excavations reveal what texts cannot say.

📜 Literary Sources

Vedic texts (Rigveda calls Bihar "Kikata"), Buddhist literature (Anguttara Nikaya, Tripitaka), Jain texts (Bhagwati Sutra), Puranas (Vishnu, Vayu, Matsya), Arthashastra by Chanakya. Each tradition preserves a different angle on the same land.

🪙 Coins & Inscriptions

Punch-marked silver coins from Patna/Purnea show early trade. Ashokan pillars in Brahmi script, Prakrit language (Lauriya Areraj, Rampurwa). Gupta copper plates from Nalanda/Gaya. Coins track empire boundaries — Kushana coins at Chirand/Buxar show their reach.

✈️ Foreign Travellers

Megasthenes (302–288 BC) → Indica → Mauryan Pataliputra. Fa-Hien (399–412 AD) → Chandragupta II's Magadha. Hiuen Tsang (637–644 AD) → Nalanda University. I-Tsing (671–695 AD) → stayed Nalanda 11 years. Each filled gaps no Indian source could.

⏳ Timeline — Prehistoric Bihar CHRONOLOGICAL
PALEOLITHIC AGE
❌ Absent from Bihar ⚠ EXAM TRAP
No Paleolithic remains found in Bihar. This is a frequent trick question — don't confuse with Mesolithic! The Old Stone Age left no trace in this region.
MESOLITHIC AGE · 12000 BC – 6000 BC
First Humans in Bihar — Munger & Nalanda
Earliest proof of human activity in Bihar. Stone tools (hand axes, cleavers) found at Paisra in Munger. Rock paintings at Nawada, Kaimur, Jamui hills — depicting hunting, dancing, animals, sun and moon. Artefacts also in Hazaribagh, Singhbhum, Santhal Pargana (now Jharkhand).
NEOLITHIC AGE · 2500 BC – 1345 BC
Chirand & Chechar — Bone Tools & Pottery
Chirand (Saran) — most important Neolithic site in Bihar; famous for bone tools. Chechar (Vaishali) — Black and red ware, ochre pottery. Also found at Taradih (Bodh Gaya), Senuwar (Rohtas), Maner (Patna). First settled agricultural communities appear.
CHALCOLITHIC AGE · 2000 BC – 700 BC
Copper + Stone = Progress
Copper tools alongside stone tools. Major sites: Chirand (Saran), Maner (Patna), Chechar-Kutubpur (Vaishali), Sonpur/Taradih (Gaya), Champa (Bhagalpur). Black and red ware pottery widespread across middle Ganga plains.
VEDIC & LATER VEDIC · 1000 BC – 600 BC
Aryans Enter Bihar — Kingdom of Videha
Aryans moved east using iron tools to clear forests. Satapatha Brahmana records Aryan arrival in North Bihar via the story of Videgha Mathava crossing river Sadanira (Gandak). Kingdom of Videha established with capital at Mithila (modern Janakpur, Nepal). Bihar called "Kikata" in Rigveda; "Magadha people" in Atharvaveda.

📝 PYQ Practice SOLVED QUESTIONS
BPSC Prelims BPSC 2015
Who among the following was a student of Nalanda University?
(a) Megasthenes
(b) Hiuen-Tsang
(c) Ibn Battuta
(d) Al-Biruni
✓ CORRECT ANSWER: (b) Hiuen-Tsang

Hiuen Tsang (637–644 AD) visited Nalanda University during Harsha's reign and stayed for about 5 years studying Buddhist philosophy. Megasthenes visited Chandragupta Maurya's court; Ibn Battuta and Al-Biruni are medieval period travellers and not connected to Nalanda.

BPSC Prelims BPSC 2004
Which source has description about administration in Patliputra?
(a) Divyavadana
(b) Arthashastra
(c) Indica
(d) Inscriptions of Asoka
✓ CORRECT ANSWER: (c) Indica

Megasthenes' Indica is the primary source describing urban administration in Patliputra — including the 30-member municipal council divided into 6 committees of 5 each. The Arthashastra describes the theory of Mauryan governance by Chanakya, while Indica is the eyewitness account by the Greek ambassador.

BPSC Prelims BPSC 2008 (Trap Q)
Pillar inscriptions in Lauriya Areraj give description in which language?
(a) Brahmi (script)
(b) Prakrit (language)
(c) Pali
(d) Both (a) and (b)
✓ CORRECT ANSWER: (b) Prakrit

⚠️ Classic trap: Brahmi is the script (writing system), while Prakrit is the language. Ashokan inscriptions at Lauriya Areraj are written IN the Brahmi SCRIPT but the LANGUAGE is Prakrit. The question asks about "language" — so answer is Prakrit. Answer (d) "Both" would be correct if the question asked about script AND language — but since it only asks "language," the answer is (b).

✅ Concept Check TEST YOURSELF
1. In which of the following books is the region of Bihar called 'Kikata'?
✓ Correct! Rigveda calls Bihar "Kikata" and its people "Vratyas." The Atharvaveda mentions "Magadha" people. Shatapatha Brahmana mentions Aryan arrival in Bihar. These are three separate texts giving different information.
✗ The answer is (B) Rigveda. It calls the Bihar region "Kikata" and people "Vratyas." Don't confuse — Atharvaveda mentions "Magadha" not "Kikata."
2. The first neolithic culture in the Ganga Valley was found at which site in Bihar?
✓ Correct! Chirand (Saran district) had the first Neolithic culture in the Ganga valley — famous for polished stone tools and bone tools. Paisra (Munger) is Mesolithic; Kumhrar is Mauryan.
✗ The answer is (B) Chirand, Saran — first Neolithic site in Ganga Valley. Paisra (Munger) is Mesolithic; Kumhrar is Mauryan period. Chirand is the standard answer for BPSC.
MAINS GS-I 150 words
Discuss the various types of sources used to reconstruct the ancient history of Bihar. How do foreign travellers' accounts supplement domestic literary and archaeological evidence?
Introduction

The ancient history of Bihar is reconstructed through three complementary source types: archaeological evidence, literary sources, and foreign accounts — each filling gaps the others cannot.

Archaeological Sources

Physical remains tell Bihar's story before writing existed. Key sites include Chirand (Saran) for Neolithic bone tools, Paisra (Munger) for Mesolithic implements, Kumhrar (Patna) for Mauryan 80-pillared hall, and Vaishali for Gupta seals. Ashokan pillars at Lauriya Areraj and Rampurwa (both in Champaran) remain among Bihar's most significant inscriptions.

Literary Sources

Vedic texts (Rigveda identifies Bihar as "Kikata"), Buddhist canons (Anguttara Nikaya lists 16 Mahajanapadas), Jain literature (Bhagwati Sutra on Licchavis), Arthashastra and Puranas collectively cover political, religious and administrative history from 1000 BC onwards.

Supplement by Foreign Accounts
  • Megasthenes (Indica): Described Pataliputra's 30-member municipal body — no Indian source details this.
  • Fa-Hien: Described Magadha's social conditions during Gupta period that Indian texts omit.
  • Hiuen Tsang: Left the only detailed account of Nalanda University's functioning — curriculum, scholars, structure.
Conclusion

Foreign accounts provide objective, contemporary observations often absent in self-referential Indian sources. Their accounts validate domestic records and add irreplaceable administrative and social detail — making them essential, not supplementary, to Bihar's ancient history reconstruction.
