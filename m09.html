MODULE 09·Medieval Bihar

Sena & Karnata Dynasties —
Twilight Before the Storm

As the Pala Buddhist empire crumbled, two Hindu dynasties rose to shape Bihar's final pre-Islamic era: the Senas with their Brahmanical revival, and the Karnatas who gave Mithila its Golden Age of literature and culture.

BPSC Special Mains GS-I 1097 AD – 1324 AD ⚠ Trap: Karnata dynasty ≠ Karnataka state. Karnata = dynasty of Mithila (N. Bihar)
🎭Story HookSTART HERE

The year is 1174 AD. In the Mithila court at Simraon (Champaran), a young poet named Vidyapati is reciting verses so beautiful they will be sung across Bengal and Bihar for the next 800 years. At the same court, the great Sanskrit composer Jyotirishwar writes the Varnaratnakar — a celebrated Maithili text that's still studied today.

The Karnata rulers of Mithila had turned their small kingdom into Bihar's most brilliant cultural moment — the Golden Age of Mithila. While empires clashed around them, they built a literary civilization that outlasted every sword and conquest.

Meanwhile, in the south, the Sena dynasty was carrying out its own cultural revolution: reviving Brahmanical Hinduism and creating the "Kulinism" caste system — controversial but powerful, shaping Bengal and Bihar's social structure for centuries.

💡 The Karnata rulers of Mithila introduced the Panji system — a genealogical register of Brahmin families still maintained in Mithila today, over 700 years later.
🧠Core Concept — Two Dynasties Side by SideWITH ANALOGY
🎭 Modern Analogy

Think of the Sena and Karnata dynasties like two regional companies operating in different parts of Bihar simultaneously. The Senas were like a Brahmanical-conservative firm in eastern Bihar, known for strict social order (Kulinism). The Karnatas were like a cultural-liberal startup in North Bihar (Mithila), investing heavily in arts, music, and literature. Both were weakened by Turkish invasion — but the Karnatas left a cultural legacy that no army could destroy.

🏛️ Sena Dynasty

Mid-11th Century – 1200 AD · Eastern Bihar & Bengal
Samantsena Founder, mid-11th C Founder of Sena dynasty. Described as Brahmakshatriya. Succeeded by son Hemantsena.
Vijayasena ~1096–1159 AD Son of Hemantsena. Brought family into limelight during his long reign of more than 60 years. Expanded Sena power significantly. Deopara inscription of Vijayasena mentions exploitation of Pala vassals by Senas in East Bihar.
Ballalsena ~1159–1179 AD Vijayasena's son. Great scholar — wrote Danasagara and Adbhutsagara. Started social movement called "Kulinism" — nobility of birth and purity of blood carefully protected. This created hierarchical sub-castes within Brahmins, Kayasthas, and Vaidyas of Bengal and Bihar. Sanokhar inscription of Ballalsena confirms expansion in East Bihar.
Lakshmansena ~1179–1206 AD Last important ruler. Succeeded at age 60. Sena power weakened by internal rebellions AND invasion of Bakhtiyar Khalji (1203 AD). Fled capital; took refuge in East Bengal's Vikrampur. NOT politically competent BUT great literary patron — court had Jayadeva (author of Gita Govinda), Halayudha (linguist), Dhoyi (author of Pavanadutam).

🎨 Karnata Dynasty of Mithila

1097 AD – 1324 AD · North Bihar (Mithila)
Nanyadeva 1097–1147 AD (Founder) Founded Karnata dynasty in Mithila in 1097 AD. Hailed from the Deccan (Karnata-Kshatriya clan). Capital: Simraon, Champaran. Apart from being a warrior, great patron of music — wrote commentary on Bharata's Natyasastra; known as Mahasamantadhipati. Conquered Nepal. Ruled ~50 years.
Gangadeva 1147–1188 AD Son of Nanyadeva. Able administrator.
Narsimhadeva 1188–1227 AD Bakhtiyar Khalji passed through Tirhut (his territory) on way to Lakhnauti in 1202. Narsimhadeva saved himself by agreeing to pay tribute to Khalji. Mithila continued to be raided by Muslims. Tibetan scholar Dharmaswamin visited his court (1234–1236 AD).
Harisimhadeva ~1295–1324 AD (Last) Last great king of Karnata dynasty. Greatest patron of art and literature. Court had Jyotirishwar (author of Varnaratnakar — celebrated Maithili text). Period of Karnata rulers = "Golden Period of Mithila." Introduced Panji Vyavastha (Panji Prabandha) — genealogical register of Brahmin families, still maintained. Defeated by Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq (1324 AD) — fled to Nepal, where he introduced worship of goddess Taleju (temple in present-day Kathmandu). After his fall, Oiniwar dynasty governed Mithila (1353–1526 AD).
⏳Timeline — Sena & Karnata DynastiesCHRONOLOGICAL
MID-11th CENTURY — SENA DYNASTY RISES
Samantsena Founds Sena Dynasty as Palas Decline
Brahmakshatriya clan from south India. Gradually expands in Bengal and eastern Bihar as Pala power weakens. Vijayasena's 60+ year reign is the cornerstone of Sena power.
1097 AD — KARNATA DYNASTY FOUNDED
Nanyadeva Founds Karnata Kingdom in Mithila
Capital at Simraon, Champaran. From Deccan (Karnataka region). Music patron — wrote commentary on Natyasastra. Conquered Nepal. Karnata ≠ Karnataka state — this dynasty ruled North Bihar.
~1159–1179 AD — BALLALSENA
Sena Peak + Kulinism + Literary Patronage
Ballalsena writes Danasagara and Adbhutsagara. Creates Kulinism caste system. Sena kingdom at cultural peak. Later son Lakshmansena hosts Jayadeva (Gita Govinda) at his court.
1202–1203 AD — BAKHTIYAR KHALJI INVASION
Both Dynasties Feel Turkish Pressure
Bakhtiyar Khalji passes through Karnata territory (Tirhut) in 1202; Narsimhadeva pays tribute. Same invasion crushes Lakshmansena's Sena kingdom — Lakshmansena flees to Vikrampur. End of Sena dynasty in Bihar.
~1295–1324 AD — HARISIMHADEVA
Golden Period of Mithila — Cultural Zenith
Jyotirishwar writes Varnaratnakar at his court. Harisimhadeva introduces Panji Vyavastha. Period = "Golden Period of Mithila." Famous for art, music, and Sanskrit/Maithili literature.
1324 AD — GHIYASUDDIN TUGHLAQ ATTACKS
End of Karnata Dynasty — Harisimhadeva Flees to Nepal
Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq defeats Harisimhadeva. He flees to Nepal; introduces Taleju goddess worship there. Tughlaq gives Tirhut to Ahmad. Oiniwar dynasty governs Mithila (1353–1526 AD).

BPSC Special — Literary Questions

Questions on Gita Govinda, Varnaratnakar, Danasagara, Panji Vyavastha, Kulinism are frequently asked. Know which text belongs to which dynasty and ruler.

Gita Govinda

Jayadeva · Lakshmansena's court (Sena dynasty)

Sanskrit lyric poem celebrating love of Radha and Krishna. One of India's greatest devotional texts. Lakshmansena was its patron despite being a politically weak king.

Varnaratnakar

Jyotirishwar · Harisimhadeva's court (Karnata dynasty)

Celebrated Maithili text. One of earliest significant works in Maithili language. Royal priest at Harisimhadeva's court. Covers language, literature, music, and social customs.

Danasagara & Adbhutsagara

Ballalsena · Sena dynasty

Works by Ballalsena himself — a rare scholar-king. Danasagara covers ritual giving (dana); Adbhutsagara deals with omens and wonders. Confirms Sena rulers' intellectual credentials.

Pavanadutam

Dhoyi · Lakshmansena's court (Sena dynasty)

Sanskrit poem by Dhoyi, composed in Lakshmansena's court. Sanskrit messenger poem tradition inspired by Kalidasa's Meghaduta.

BPSC PrelimsBPSC 2017
The author of Rajaniti Ratnakara is:
(a) Chandesvara
(b) Vidyapati
(c) Jyotiresvara
(d) Haribrahmendra
✓ CORRECT: (a) Chandesvara

Chandesvara wrote the Rajaniti Ratnakara — an important treatise on statecraft (Rajniti) from the Mithila/Karnata era. Note: Jyotirishwar (Jyotiresvara) wrote the Varnaratnakar — don't confuse these two. Vidyapati wrote devotional Maithili poetry. The Karnata era produced multiple important Mithila texts.

(a) Mithila
(b) Bhojpur

MAINS GS-I150 words
Describe the cultural contributions of the Karnata dynasty of Mithila. Why is their period called the "Golden Period of Mithila"?
Introduction

The Karnata dynasty (1097–1324 AD), founded by Nanyadeva in Mithila (North Bihar), is remembered not for military conquest but for transforming Mithila into ancient India's greatest centre of Sanskrit and Maithili culture.

Why "Golden Period of Mithila"
  • Literary Patronage: Harisimhadeva's court hosted Jyotirishwar (author of Varnaratnakar — early Maithili masterpiece) and Chandesvara (Rajaniti Ratnakara)
  • Music: Nanyadeva himself wrote a commentary on Bharata's Natyasastra — showing kings as active scholars, not mere patrons
  • Social Institution: Harisimhadeva introduced Panji Vyavastha — the genealogical register of Brahmin families, still maintained in Mithila today (700+ years later)
  • Religious Influence: Even in defeat, Harisimhadeva spread Mithila culture — he introduced Taleju goddess worship in Nepal, which became central to Nepalese royal tradition
Modern Relevance

The Maithili language and Mithila art (Madhubani paintings) that define North Bihar's cultural identity today owe their early flowering to Karnata patronage. The Panji system still serves as a living genealogical archive — a 700-year-old institution from a 227-year dynasty.

Conclusion

The Karnata period represents Bihar's truth: that cultural legacy often outlasts military power. The Karnata dynasty was militarily weak but culturally transformative — their Golden Period of Mithila lives on in language, art, and social institutions still visible today.
