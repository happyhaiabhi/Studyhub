MODULE 15·Modern Bihar

European Entry in Bihar —
The Saltpetre Connection

Five European powers entered Bihar in the 17th–18th centuries — all chasing the same thing: saltpetre. Bihar's soil literally fuelled Europe's wars. This module covers who came, when, where, and how the East India Company eventually dominated all rivals.

BPSC Prelims Mains GS-I ⚠ Trap: Portuguese = first Europeans. EIC first factory = Alamganj 1620, NOT 1651. Dutch factory = Patna College northern building.
🎭Story HookSTART HERE

The year is 1620 AD. A small group of English merchants arrives in a place called Alamganj on the banks of the Ganga near Patna. They are not here for trade in silk or spices — they are here for something far less glamorous: saltpetre.

Saltpetre — potassium nitrate — is the key ingredient in gunpowder. And Bihar produces it in abundance. Its Gangetic soil is literally soaked in it. European armies are fighting wars constantly, and they need gunpowder to fight them. Whoever controls Bihar's saltpetre controls Europe's weapons supply chains.

The English establish a factory. Within a year, they close it — the trade is not yet profitable enough. But 31 years later, they're back, and this time they never leave. By 1765, they will control not just Patna's saltpetre but the entire revenue of Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa.

💡 Bihar's soil funded European wars for 150 years. The region that gave the world Nalanda and Ashoka's empire also — inadvertently — fuelled the Age of Gunpowder.
🧠Five Europeans — Country by CountryWITH ANALOGY
⚓ Modern Analogy

Think of 17th-century Bihar as a lithium mine in today's world — except instead of electric cars, it was gunpowder. Every European power wanted access. They competed, fought, and negotiated, just like modern tech companies over rare-earth minerals. The British East India Company eventually won not by having the best saltpetre, but by having the best military and diplomatic strategy.

🇳🇱 Dutch

1632 AD · PATNA COLLEGE NORTH BUILDING
Factory:1632 — established at northern part of present Patna College
Rights:1662 — Aurangzeb granted trade rights to Dutch in Bihar, Bengal, Odisha
Traveller:1665 — Tavernier came to Patna; then Chhapra (major saltpetre centre)
Defeat:November 1759 — British defeated Dutch at Battle of Bedara
End:1781 — British captured Dutch warehouse. 1784 — returned. Dutch influence ended.

🇫🇷 French

1664 · SALTPETRE RIVALRY WITH DUTCH
Company:French East India Company founded 1664 by François Marten
Factory:1734 — established trading centre + warehouse at Patna
Rival:Constant struggle with Dutch over saltpetre trade rights
End:After Battle of Plassey (1757), British captured Chandannagar; then expelled French from Patna + seized all French warehouses

French East India Company (founded 1664 by François Marten) sets up trading centre in Patna. Rivals the Dutch for saltpetre.

After Plassey, British capture Chandannagar and seize French warehouses in Patna. French commercial presence in Bihar ends permanently.
1759 AD

British defeat Dutch at Battle of Bedara (November 1759). Dutch commercial power in Bihar ends. 1784: Patna warehouse returned but influence gone.

Danes establish saltpetre-processing factory at Nepali Kothi. Lasted till Denmark sold all India settlements (1845). Small but persistent presence.

MAINS GS-I · 150 WORDS
Analyse the role of saltpetre in attracting European trading companies to Bihar. How did the East India Company eventually emerge as the dominant European power?
Introduction

Bihar's Gangetic plains produced the world's finest saltpetre (potassium nitrate), the key ingredient in gunpowder. This made 17th–18th century Bihar strategically vital to every European power engaged in warfare.

Saltpetre as the Magnet
  • All five European powers — Portuguese, Dutch, French, Danish, British — established factories primarily to access Bihar's saltpetre
  • Chhapra was the major saltpetre refinery centre; Hajipur, Tirhut, Saran, Purnea also produced it in quantity
  • Dutch-French rivalry over saltpetre rights was intense throughout the 17th century
EIC's Rise to Dominance
  • 1620: First factory at Alamganj (closed 1621 — premature attempt)
  • 1651: Permanent base at Gulzar Bagh; gradually built trade networks
  • 1757 (Plassey): Expelled French from Bihar
  • 1759 (Bedara): Defeated Dutch commercially
  • 1764 (Buxar): Gained Diwani rights — becoming revenue collectors of Bihar
Conclusion

The EIC succeeded not through superior trade goods but through superior military strategy and diplomatic manipulation — defeating each rival in turn and using revenue rights (Diwani, 1765) to convert a trade post into political control. Bihar's saltpetre literally fuelled both Europe's wars and Britain's Indian empire.
