MODULE 06·Ancient Bihar

Ashoka —
From Kalinga to Dhamma

One war. 100,000 dead. And a king so shattered by what he saw that he transformed from the most feared warrior-emperor in Asia into the world's first great proponent of non-violence, welfare governance, and religious tolerance.

BPSC Special Mains GS-I 273 BC – 232 BC ⚠ Devanampiya ≠ always Ashoka; Interregnum 273-269 BC; Rock Edict XIII = Kalinga
🎭Story HookSTART HERE

It is 261 BC. The Mauryan army has just conquered Kalinga (modern Odisha). The numbers: 100,000 killed in battle. 150,000 deported. Several times more died of disease and starvation.

Emperor Ashoka walks through the battlefield. He had conquered before — more fiercely than any Mauryan before him. But something about this battlefield is different. Perhaps it's the sheer scale of death. Perhaps it's a Buddhist monk he meets in the carnage. Perhaps it's the face of someone's grandfather lying in the mud, staring at the sky.

Ashoka returns to Pataliputra a changed man. He describes what happened in Rock Edict XIII — one of the most remarkable royal confessions in world history: "A hundred thousand were killed, many times that number perished, and many times still that number were taken captive. There, Beloved-of-the-Gods feels remorse."

He never fights a war of aggression again. He builds hospitals for humans and animals. He plants trees along roads. He sends missionaries across Asia. And on pillars in Bihar's Champaran — at Lauriya Areraj and Rampurwa — he writes messages to his people that are still readable today, 2,300 years later.

💡 Ashoka is the only emperor in history who gave up conquest after winning — and built a welfare state instead. His story is as relevant today as in 261 BC.

☸Ashoka's Dhamma — Key PrinciplesWELFARE STATE
🕊 Non-Violence (Ahimsa)
Stopped animal sacrifices. Banned hunting of many animals. Became vegetarian. Built veterinary hospitals alongside human hospitals.
🤝 Religious Tolerance
Respected all religions — Buddhism, Brahmanism, Jainism, Ajivikas. Rock Edict XII says: "All sects deserve reverence for one reason or another."
👴 Respect for Elders
Reverence for parents, teachers, elders. Generosity to brahmanas and monks. Care for servants and slaves — ahead of his time.
🌳 Welfare Works
Planted banyan trees along roads. Built rest houses (Dharmashalas). Dug wells. Built hospitals. His welfare works extended to Sri Lanka and Central Asia.
📜 Dhamma Mahamattas
Appointed special officers called Dhamma Mahamattas to spread Dhamma principles among the people and to monitor compliance.
🌏 Global Spread
3rd Buddhist Council (250 BC, Patliputra) → missionaries sent to Sri Lanka (Mahendra, Sanghamitra), SE Asia, W Asia, Egypt, Macedonia, Mediterranean Europe.

⏳Ashoka's Reign TimelineCHRONOLOGICAL
273 BC
Ashoka Succeeds Bindusara 4-yr interregnum
Bindusara died 273 BC. Ashoka fought succession war against brother Susima. Sworn in (coronated) 4 years later in 269 BC. Per Dipavamsa/Mahavamsa — killed 99 brothers, spared Vitashoka/Tissa. Was earlier Governor of Ujjain under Bindusara.
261 BC
Kalinga War — The Turning Point
Mentioned in Major Rock Edict XIII. 100,000 killed; 150,000 deported. Ashoka embraces Buddhism. Monk Upagupta influenced him. Becomes Dhammasoka.
250 BC
3rd Buddhist Council — Patliputra, Bihar
Patron: Ashoka. President: Moggaliputta Tissa. Compiled Abhidhamma Pitaka. Buddhist missions sent to Sri Lanka (son Mahendra + daughter Sanghamitra), SE Asia, W Asia, Mediterranean. Most important event in Buddhist world history.
~250 BC
84,000 Stupas Built
Ashoka distributed relics of Buddha and built 84,000 stupas across his empire. Also built stone pillars, dharmashalas (rest houses), hospitals, planted trees on roads. Pillar inscriptions in Bihar (Lauriya Areraj, Rampurwa, Vaishali).
232 BC
Ashoka Dies — Empire Begins Declining
After Ashoka, empire split — Western part (Kunala's descendants) fell to Bactrian invasion. Eastern part (Dasharatha/Samprati) survived briefly at Pataliputra. Last Mauryan Brihadratha killed by Pushyamitra Shunga in 185 BC.

In which Rock Edict did Ashoka mention about the Kalinga War?
(a) Rock Edict XIII
(b) Rock Edict XII
(c) Rock Edict XI
(d) Rock Edict X
✓ CORRECT: (a) Rock Edict XIII

Major Rock Edict XIII is Ashoka's most famous edict — it describes his grief and remorse after the Kalinga War. It mentions 100,000 killed, 150,000 deported, and many more who suffered. It contains Ashoka's vow to pursue conquest by Dhamma rather than by arms. Rock Edict XII is about religious tolerance; Rock Edict XI is about Dhamma. XIII = Kalinga = most emotional, most important.

✅Concept CheckTEST YOURSELF
1. Ashoka is mentioned as "King of Magadha" in which specific inscription — the only place where the name "Ashoka" is explicitly written?
✓ Correct! The Bhabru Inscription (found in Rajasthan) explicitly mentions the name "Ashoka" — other inscriptions use titles like "Devanampiya" and "Priyadarshee." The Hathigumpha is Kharavela's inscription (Odisha), not Ashoka's. Rock Edict XIII describes Kalinga war but doesn't use "Ashoka" by name.
✗ Answer: (B) Bhabru Inscription. This is a classic exam trap — most people think Rock Edict XIII or pillar inscriptions name Ashoka. But "Devanampiya" appears everywhere; "Ashoka" is explicitly named only in the Bhabru Inscription.
2. The Nagarjuni Hills inscription (near Bodh Gaya, Bihar) belongs to which Mauryan ruler — NOT Ashoka?
✓ Correct! The Nagarjuni Hills inscription is of Mauryan King Dasharatha — Ashoka's grandson — who dedicated caves to the Ajivikas. Barabar caves were dedicated by Ashoka himself; Nagarjuni Hills (nearby) by Dasharatha. Samprati was a patron of Jainism. This is a favourite BPSC trap.
✗ Answer: (B) Dasharatha. Dasharatha was Ashoka's grandson who ruled the eastern part of the Mauryan Empire after Ashoka's death. The Nagarjuni Hills inscription (near Jehanabad, Bihar) is his — dedicating caves to the Ajivikas, continuing Ashoka's tradition of religious patronage.
MAINS GS-I150 words
"Ashoka's Kalinga War is as significant as the war itself — for what it triggered." Discuss Ashoka's transformation from a conquering emperor to a welfare ruler, and assess his legacy for modern governance.
Introduction

The Kalinga War (261 BC) is unique in world history — not for its military outcome, but for triggering the most profound personal and political transformation any emperor has documented in his own words.

The Transformation
  • Pre-Kalinga Ashoka: Aggressive conqueror; Rock Edict XIII describes him as a ruler who "sent armed expeditions"
  • Post-Kalinga: Embraces Buddhism; abandons Digvijaya; appoints Dhamma Mahamattas; sends Buddhist missions globally
  • Welfare measures: Hospitals for humans and animals; roadside trees; rest houses; wells; religious tolerance across all sects
Legacy for Modern Governance
  • Secularism: Rock Edict XII — "All sects deserve reverence" — anticipates modern religious pluralism
  • Welfare state: State responsibility for subjects' well-being, not just military security
  • Non-violence: Ahimsa as state policy — still referenced in India's Constitution and Gandhi's movement
  • National symbols: Ashoka Chakra on India's flag; Lion Capital at Sarnath = India's national emblem
Conclusion

Ashoka's legacy transcends his empire. His pillars in Bihar's Champaran carry messages that resonate today: tolerance, welfare, non-violence, and the dignity of all living beings — principles as urgent in 2025 as in 261 BC.
