MODULE 08·Medieval Bihar

Pala Dynasty —
Buddhist Renaissance

After 600 years of Gupta glory, Bihar fell into anarchy. Then the people themselves elected a king. The Pala dynasty rose from chaos to create Bihar's last great Buddhist empire — funding Nalanda, building Vikramshila, and dominating north India for 400 years.

BPSC Prelims Mains GS-I 750 AD – 1150 AD ⚠ Trap: Vikramshila = Dharampala (NOT Gopala); Munger capital = Devapala (NOT Dharampala)
🎭Story HookSTART HERE

The year is 750 AD. Bihar and Bengal are in chaos. The Gupta empire has been gone for 200 years. No king has lasted more than a decade. Rival chiefs fight each other while peasants starve — a condition the Buddhists called "matsya-nyaya", the law of the fish: the big devour the small.

Then something extraordinary happens. The people of Magadha and Bengal do something almost unheard of in ancient India: they vote. They assemble, deliberate, and elect a man named Gopala as their king. The Khalimpur Copper Plate inscription records this democratic election — recognised as one of the first in South Asia since the Licchavi republic of Vaishali.

Gopala founds the Pala dynasty — and for the next four centuries, Bihar will once again be a centre of Buddhist scholarship, with two of the greatest universities the ancient world has ever seen.

💡 The Pala dynasty is the only Indian dynasty founded by popular democratic election — a fact almost no textbook emphasises enough.
🧠Core Concept — Key Pala RulersWITH ANALOGY
🏫 Modern Analogy

Think of the Pala kings as university chancellors who also happened to be warlords. Their greatest legacy wasn't military conquest — it was building Vikramshila University (Dharampala) and maintaining Nalanda. They fought a three-way war (with Pratiharas and Rashtrakutas) for control of Kanauj — like three tech giants fighting over one market. But what made them truly great was patronising the world's greatest libraries and monasteries.

Gopala
FOUNDER
750–770 AD
  • Founded Pala dynasty c.750 AD by democratic election — people chose him to end matsya-nyaya (anarchy)
  • Khalimpur Copper Plate inscription records this election
  • Extended control over all of Bengal and parts of Bihar (Magadha)
  • Buddhist patron — founded Odantapura Vihara at Bihar Sharif (ancient Bihar Sharif = Odantapuri)
  • Also built a monastery near Nalanda
Dharampala
⭐ EMPIRE BUILDER
770–810 AD
  • Greatest Pala ruler — empire reached its first peak under him
  • Fought Tripartite Struggle with Gurjara Pratiharas (Vatsaraja, Nagabhatta II) and Rashtrakutas (Dhruva, Govinda III) for control of Kanauj
  • After Govinda III withdrew to Deccan, Dharampala captured Kanauj, installed Chakrayudha, and earned title "Uttarapathaswami" (Lord of the North)
  • Organised grand Darbar at Kanauj — attended by kings of Nepal, Bhoja, Matsya, Kuru, Yadu, Gandhara and others
  • First Pala to use full imperial title: Maharajadhiraja Parameshvara Paramabhattaraka
  • Founded Vikramshila Mahavihara at Antichak village, Bhagalpur district — great rival to Nalanda
  • Built Sonapura Vihara in North Bengal
  • Gave 200 villages for maintenance of Nalanda University
  • Tolerant of all religions — image of four-faced Mahadeva installed at Bodh Gaya during his reign
Devapala
PEAK EXPANSION
810–850 AD
  • Empire reached further heights — conquered Utkala (Orissa) and Pragjyotisha (Assam/Kamrupa)
  • Capital: Munger — his inscriptions found at Ghosrawan, Hilsa, Nalanda, Munger
  • Maintained friendly relations with Balaputradeva, king of Suvarnadvipa (Sumatra/SE Asia) — who built monastery at Nalanda; Devapala granted 5 villages for its maintenance
  • Munger Copper Plate inscription (of Devapala) states Dharampala left successor a kingdom free from troubles
Mahipala I
SECOND FOUNDER
988–1038 AD
  • Known as "second founder of Pala dynasty" — revived Pala power after period of decline
  • Reign (988 AD): Bihar and Bengal invaded by Rajendra Chola I of Chola dynasty (south Indian emperor) around 1023 AD
  • Mahipala I fought Rajendra Chola and was defeated. Died same year (1023 AD)
  • After him: Nayapala, Vigrahapala III, Mahipala II (faced Kaivarta revolt by Sandhyakar Nandi's patron), Rampala (last significant ruler — restored Pala fortunes temporarily)
  • Rampala = last ruler; Ramacharita by Sandhyakara Nandi describes his reign
⏳Timeline — Pala DynastyCHRONOLOGICAL
750 AD — DEMOCRATIC FOUNDING
Gopala Elected King — Pala Dynasty Begins
People elect Gopala to end matsya-nyaya (anarchy). Founds Odantapura Vihara (Bihar Sharif). Expands into Bengal and Bihar (Magadha). First democratic election since Vaishali Licchavi republic.
770–810 AD — DHARAMPALA
Tripartite Struggle + Vikramshila University Founded
Fights Pratiharas and Rashtrakutas for Kanauj. Captures Kanauj; title Uttarapathaswami. Grand Darbar at Kanauj. Founds Vikramshila at Antichak, Bhagalpur. Gives 200 villages to Nalanda.
810–850 AD — DEVAPALA
Maximum Expansion — Capital at Munger
Conquers Orissa and Assam. Capital at Munger. Balaputradeva of SE Asia builds monastery at Nalanda; Devapala grants 5 villages. Inscriptions at Ghosrawan, Hilsa, Nalanda, Munger.
850–988 AD — LATER PALAS
Gradual Decline — Internal Troubles
Vigrahapala I, Narayanapala, Rajyapala, Gopala II, Vigrahapala II. Pratihara and Rashtrakuta pressure continues. Territory shrinks. Narayanapala loses ground to Gurjara-Pratiharas.
988–1038 AD — MAHIPALA I
"Second Founder" — Brief Revival
Revives Pala power. Defeated by Rajendra Chola I (1023 AD). Dies same year. His revival inspired the Pala renaissance that continued under Rampala.
1038–1150 AD — FINAL PALAS
Mahipala II, Rampala — End of Pala Rule
Mahipala II faces Kaivarta revolt (Sandhyakar). Rampala = last significant ruler; temporarily restored fortunes; Ramacharita describes his reign. After Rampala, Pala empire broke up completely.
🔄Flowchart — Pala Legacy & DeclineCAUSE → EVENT → RESULT
Cause of Rise

Political Anarchy (Matsya-Nyaya)

Post-Gupta power vacuum. No stable dynasty. People elect Gopala democratically to end chaos.

Peak Achievements

Buddhist Scholarship + North India Dominance

Vikramshila (Dharampala), Nalanda patronage, Tripartite Struggle, Uttarapathaswami title, SE Asia Buddhist connections.

Cause of Decline

Three-Way Pressure + Turkish Invasion

Pratihara + Rashtrakuta pressure eroded power. Rajendra Chola attack from south. Finally Bakhtiyar Khalji destroys universities (1197 AD).

📝PYQ PracticeSOLVED
BPSC PrelimsBPSC 2009
Which Pala ruler organised a grand Darbar in Kanauj?
(a) Gopala
(b) Dharmapala
(c) Devpala
(d) None of the above
✓ CORRECT: (b) Dharmapala

Dharampala captured Kanauj, installed Chakrayudha as its ruler, and organised a grand Darbar (royal assembly) there. This Darbar was attended by kings of Nepal, Bhoja, Matsya, Kuru, Yadu, Gandhara and others — symbolising Dharampala's supremacy over north India. He earned the title "Uttarapathaswami" (Lord of the Northern Path) for this achievement.

BPSC PrelimsBPSC 2018
The ruler of Suvarnabhumi who founded a Buddhist monastery at Nalanda and requested Devapala through his ambassador to grant five villages for its maintenance was:
(a) Dharanindra
(b) Sangramadhananjaya
(c) Balaputradeva
(d) Chudamanivarmana
✓ CORRECT: (c) Balaputradeva

Balaputradeva was the ruler of Suvarnabhumi (Sumatra/Southeast Asia) who built a Buddhist monastery at Nalanda during Devapala's reign. He sent an ambassador to request Devapala to grant five villages for the monastery's maintenance — which Devapala approved. This shows the international reach of Pala-era Nalanda as a centre of Buddhist learning far beyond India.

BPSC PrelimsBPSC (MCQ Bank)
Who established Vikramshila University?
(a) Adityasena
(b) Madhavagupta
(c) Dharmapala
(d) Devapala
✓ CORRECT: (c) Dharmapala

Dharampala founded the Vikramshila Mahavihara at Antichak village near Kahalgaon in the Bhagalpur district of Bihar. This university became Nalanda's rival in importance, specialising in Tantric Buddhism. Excavations at Antichak since 1962 have confirmed its location. It was destroyed by Bakhtiyar Khalji along with Nalanda and Odantapura. Option (d) Devapala ruled from Munger but did NOT found Vikramshila — he patronised Nalanda instead.

✅Concept CheckTEST YOURSELF
1. Munger was the capital of which Pala ruler?
✓ Correct! Devapala (810–850 AD) made Munger his capital. This is a frequently tested BPSC fact. Don't confuse with Dharampala who preceded him — Dharampala is known for Vikramshila and Kanauj Darbar, not for Munger capital.
✗ Answer is (C) Devapala. He ruled from Munger (810–850 AD). His inscriptions have been found at Ghosrawan, Hilsa, Nalanda, and Munger. Dharampala is associated with Vikramshila and Kanauj; Gopala founded the dynasty without a fixed major capital.
2. Who was the last ruler of the Pala dynasty?
✓ Correct! Rampala was the last ruler of the Pala dynasty (and last significant one). The Ramacharita composed by Sandhyakara Nandi describes how Rampala suppressed the Kaivarta revolt and temporarily restored Pala power, before the empire finally broke up after him.
✗ Answer is (C) Rampala. He was the last significant Pala ruler. After Rampala, the restored Pala empire broke up completely. The Ramacharita by Sandhyakara Nandi is written about him. Mahipala II was penultimate — he faced the Kaivarta revolt.
MAINS GS-I150 words
The Pala dynasty played a crucial role in preserving and spreading Buddhism across Asia. Discuss their contributions to education and religion with special reference to Bihar.
Introduction

The Pala dynasty (750–1150 AD), founded democratically in Bihar, became Buddhism's last great royal patron in India — preserving and exporting Buddhist knowledge during a critical period when it was declining elsewhere.

Educational Contributions
  • Vikramshila Mahavihara: Founded by Dharampala at Antichak, Bhagalpur — specialised in Tantric Buddhism; became Nalanda's equal in prestige
  • Nalanda: Palas gave 200 villages (Dharampala) and 5 villages (Devapala) for its maintenance — keeping it operational for centuries
  • Odantapura Vihara: Founded by Gopala at Bihar Sharif — a major centre of learning
  • International students came from China, Tibet, SE Asia to these Bihar institutions
Religious Contributions
  • Followers of Mahayana and Tantric Buddhism — patronised Buddhist art (Pala-Sena school of art at Gaya, Nalanda, Nawada)
  • International outreach: Devapala's correspondence with Balaputradeva of Suvarnabhumi (SE Asia) spread Pala-style Buddhism to Indonesia
  • Tibet received many Pala-era Buddhist texts and teachers — Pala Buddhism shaped Tibetan Buddhism permanently
Conclusion

The Pala dynasty's greatest legacy is not military but intellectual. When Bakhtiyar Khalji burned Nalanda and Vikramshila in 1197–1203 AD, he destroyed 400 years of Pala-patronised Buddhist scholarship — but the knowledge had already spread across Asia through Pala-era students and texts.
