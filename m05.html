MODULE 05·Ancient Bihar

Mauryan Empire —
Rise & Administration

321 BC. A young man from Pataliputra overthrows the mightiest dynasty in India. With a genius advisor, an iron will, and the world's largest army, Chandragupta Maurya builds what no Indian had built before — a pan-Indian empire, governed from Bihar's capital.

BPSC Prelims Mains GS-I 321 BC – 185 BC ⚠ Bindusara's Greek names; Arthashastra vs Indica distinction
🎭Story HookSTART HERE

It is 305 BC. The Greek general Seleucus Nicator — one of Alexander the Great's most powerful successors — has invaded India. He expects to reconquer what Alexander had left. Instead, he meets a 20-year-old emperor named Chandragupta Maurya.

The battle is swift and decisive. Seleucus loses. In the peace treaty that follows, he gives Chandragupta four entire satrapies (including Afghanistan) and sends his daughter in marriage. In return, Chandragupta sends him 500 war elephants. Seleucus uses those elephants to become the most powerful man in the Middle East.

As part of the deal, Seleucus sends his ambassador Megasthenes to live in Pataliputra. Megasthenes writes Indica — the most detailed foreign account of ancient India's administration. He describes Pataliputra as "Palibothra" — a city of magnificent wooden palaces, busy markets, and a 30-member municipal council so efficient that a Greek could only stare in admiration.

💡 Fun Fact: Chandragupta Maurya is the only Indian king who defeated a successor of Alexander the Great in pitched battle — and got paid land for it!
🧠Three Mauryan EmperorsCORE CONCEPT
🏗️ Modern Analogy

The Mauryan Empire is like a three-generation family business. Chandragupta = the founder who builds it from scratch, takes enormous risks. Bindusara = the competent second-generation who maintains and expands slightly. Ashoka = the third-generation who transforms the company's entire purpose from profit to purpose — and in doing so, creates its most lasting legacy.

👑
Chandragupta Maurya
321 BC – 298 BC
FOUNDER
Origin: Per Matsya Purana — mother Mura was a Shudra woman in Nanda court. Per Buddhist tradition — belonged to Moriya Kshatriya clan. Called "Vrishala" in Mudrarakshasa.
Mentor: Chanakya (also Kautilya/Vishnugupta) — writer of Arthashastra. Chanakya masterminded the overthrow of Dhana Nanda (321 BC).
Greek encounter: Defeated Seleucus Nicator (305 BC). Peace treaty = got Afghanistan + Balochistan. Seleucus sent Megasthenes as ambassador. Megasthenes wrote Indica — describes Pataliputra as "Palibothra." Army: 6 lakh infantry, 30,000 cavalry, 9,000 elephants.
End of reign: Per Jain sources (Parishishtaparvan) — embraced Jainism, abdicated for son Bindusara, went to Shravanabelagola with Jain monk Bhadrabahu, died by sallekhana (fasting to death).
⚔️
Bindusara
298 BC – 273 BC
EXPANDER
Greek names: Called Amitrochates or Amitraghata ("slayer of enemies") by Greek writers. Vayu Purana = Mudrasar. Jain texts = Singhsen/Bindupala. Jain text Rajvalli-Katha = Seemseri.
Expansion: Expanded empire across most of India except extreme south and east. Himalayas in north to Assam in east; Balochistan and Afghanistan in west.
Diplomacy: Syrian King Antiochus I sent Deimachus as ambassador. Ptolemy II Philadelphus of Egypt sent Dionysius to his court. Maintained strong Greek trade relations.
Religion: Supported Ajivikas (religious sect). Appointed his son Ashoka as Governor of Ujjain. His other famous son was Susima (elder, who lost the succession war).
⏳Mauryan Empire TimelineCHRONOLOGICAL
321 BC
Chandragupta + Chanakya Overthrow Dhana Nanda
Maurya dynasty founded at Magadha (Pataliputra). First pan-Indian empire begins. Chanakya's Arthashastra becomes the governance manual. Capital: Pataliputra (modern Patna).
305 BC
War with Seleucus Nicator — Chandragupta Wins
Seleucus (Alexander's general) invades India, loses to Chandragupta. Peace treaty: Seleucus gives 4 satrapies + daughter. Chandragupta gives 500 elephants. Megasthenes sent as ambassador → writes Indica.
298 BC
Chandragupta Abdicates → Bindusara Succeeds
Chandragupta embraces Jainism (per Jain sources), abdicates, goes to Shravanabelagola with monk Bhadrabahu, dies by fasting. Bindusara expands empire south. Called Amitrochates by Greeks.
273 BC
Ashoka Succeeds Bindusara — After 4-Year War
Ashoka fought a succession war after Bindusara's death in 273 BC. He was crowned (sworn in) after 4 years interregnum, in 269 BC. He was governor of Ujjain before becoming emperor. Per Dipavamsa/Mahavamsa — killed 99 brothers, spared Vitashoka/Tissa.
261 BC
Kalinga War — Everything Changes
Ashoka fights Kalinga (Odisha). Massive casualties. Rock Edict XIII describes his remorse. He converts to Buddhism, becomes Dhammasoka. This is covered in full detail in Module 06.
185 BC
Last Mauryan — Brihadratha Assassinated
Pushyamitra Shunga (Commander-in-Chief) kills last Mauryan king Brihadratha during military parade. Shunga dynasty begins. After Ashoka, the empire declined rapidly under weak rulers: Kunala, Dasharatha, Samprati, Salisuka, Devavarman, Satadhanvan, Brihadratha.

📊Mauryan Administration — Key FeaturesEXAM TABLE
👑 Central Administration
18 Tirthas/Mahamatyas (senior officials): ministers, head priest, commander-in-chief, crown prince — paid in silver coins. Empire divided into 5 provinces, each with a Governor.
⚖️ Judiciary
Two types: Dharmasthiyas (civil courts) and Kantakasodhanas (criminal courts). Rajjukas = land measurement + district control officers. Gramani = village head (Sarpanch).
🏙️ Municipal (Pataliputra)
Per Megasthenes: 30-member council divided into 6 committees of 5 each. Committees handled: industries, foreigners, births/deaths, trade, manufacturing, taxes on goods sold.
💰 Economy
Managed by 27 Adhyakshas (superintendents). Coins: silver (Panas), copper (Mashaka), gold (Niskha). 18 Guilds/Srenis organized labour. Punch-marked coins found in Patna, Purnea.
🏘️ Local Administration
Gopas = officers in charge of villages. Gramani = village Sarpanch. District controlled by Rajjukas. Provinces subdivided into districts, then villages as smallest units.
🛣️ Trade Routes
Internal: East-West route Taxila→Patliputra; Sravasti→Rajagriha. External via Tamluk (Tamralipta) on east coast; Broach, Sopara on west coast. Megasthenes praised Mauryan roads.

BPSC PrelimsBPSC 2003
Important evidence of Maurya period have been found at which place in Bihar?
(a) Sasaram
(b) Rajgir
(c) Kumhrar (Patna)
(d) Champa
✓ CORRECT: (c) Kumhrar, Patna

The 80-pillared hall of Chandragupta Maurya was excavated at Kumhrar near Patna. Excavated by Spooner (1912-13) near Bulandi Bagh. Pillars made of fine sandstone from Chunar quarries. This is the most important Mauryan evidence site in Bihar. Rajgir is pre-Mauryan capital; Sasaram has Sher Shah's tomb; Champa was Anga's capital.

BPSC PrelimsBPSC 2004
'Mauryan administration in Patliputra was run by 30 citizens divided into 6 committees.' Who made this statement?
(a) Megasthenes
(b) Kautilya
(c) Nagarjuna
(d) Vasubandhu
✓ CORRECT: (a) Megasthenes

Megasthenes (Greek ambassador in Chandragupta's court) described this in his book Indica. He described Pataliputra's 30-member municipal council divided into 6 committees of 5 each — handling industries, foreigners, births/deaths, trade, manufacturing, and tax collection. This is the most famous administrative detail from the Mauryan period.

BPSC PrelimsBPSC 2008
Who was the last ruler of the Maurya Empire?
(a) Brihadratha
(b) Pushyamitra
(c) Vasumitra
(d) Agnimitra
✓ CORRECT: (a) Brihadratha

Brihadratha was the last Mauryan ruler, assassinated in 185 BC by Pushyamitra Shunga (his own Commander-in-Chief) during a military parade. Pushyamitra then founded the Shunga dynasty. Note: Pushyamitra, Vasumitra, and Agnimitra are all Shunga dynasty rulers — not Mauryan.

✅Concept CheckTEST YOURSELF
1. According to Chanakya's Saptanga Theory, 'Kosa' refers to which element of the state?
✓ Correct! Kosa = Treasury (finances). Remember the 7 Saptanga elements: Svamin (king), Amatya (ministers), Janapada (people/territory), Durg (fort), Kosa (treasury), Bala (army), Mitra (ally). Bala = army; Durg = fort; Amatya = ministers.
✗ Answer: (C) Treasury. Kosa = treasury/finances. Bala = army; Durg = fort; Amatya = ministers. The mnemonic S-A-J-D-K-B-M: K is 5th = Kosa = treasury.
2. Chandragupta Maurya embraced Jainism towards the end of his life and according to Jain sources, went to which place to die by fasting (sallekhana)?
✓ Correct! Per Jain sources (Parishishtaparvan), Chandragupta Maurya embraced Jainism, abdicated in favour of Bindusara, and went to Shravanabelagola in Karnataka with Jain monk Bhadrabahu. He died there by sallekhana (fasting to death). This is specifically mentioned in Jain texts — not found in Buddhist or Brahmanical sources.
✗ Answer: (C) Shravanabelagola. This is a famous Jain pilgrimage site in Karnataka. Per Jain sources, Chandragupta went there with monk Bhadrabahu and died by fasting. This is a BPSC favourite — Jain sources specifically mention Shravanabelagola.
MAINS GS-I150 words
Analyse the administrative system of the Mauryan Empire. How did Chanakya's Arthashastra shape the governance of the world's first large-scale empire?
Introduction

The Mauryan Empire (321–185 BC), based at Pataliputra (modern Patna), created the first systematised administrative framework for a large-scale state in ancient India — guided by Chanakya's Arthashastra.

Theoretical Framework — Arthashastra

Chanakya's Arthashastra (15 sections) provided the theoretical basis: Saptanga Theory identified 7 essential state elements (Svamin, Amatya, Janapada, Durg, Kosa, Bala, Mitra). This framework influenced Indian statecraft for centuries and is considered one of the world's earliest works of political science.

Administrative Structure
  • Central: Emperor + 18 Tirthas (senior officials); 4-province structure with Governors (Kumaras from royal family)
  • Local: Rajjukas (land measurement/districts); Gopas (village officers); Gramani (village head)
  • Economic: 27 Adhyakshas managed economy; 18 Guilds organized labour; 3-type currency system
  • Judicial: Dharmasthiyas (civil) and Kantakasodhanas (criminal) courts
  • Municipal: Pataliputra had 30-member council in 6 committees (per Megasthenes)
Conclusion

The Mauryan administrative system was centuries ahead of its time — a blend of theory (Arthashastra) and practice (Megasthenes' eyewitness accounts). Its centralised structure with local flexibility became the model for future Indian empires, including the Guptas, and echoes in modern district administration.
